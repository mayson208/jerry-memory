# History AI YouTube Shorts — Practical Style Guide

**Research Date:** 2026-03-24
**Based On:** RelaxHistoryAI channel analysis + broader AI history Shorts ecosystem research
**Purpose:** Actionable guide for a video editor or AI system producing history Shorts in this style

---

## Part 1: What Makes This Genre Work

Before getting into technical specs, understand the psychological mechanic:

**History Shorts work because of the gap between what you expect and what you get.**

The best performing videos in this niche give the viewer something they didn't know they were missing: a photorealistic face of someone they've only seen in stiff portraits, a city block from 1400s Rome that looks like you could walk into it, a law from ancient Babylon that sounds like it was written yesterday.

The emotion you're aiming for: **"I had no idea."** Or more precisely, that feeling of suddenly seeing something familiar as if for the first time.

---

## Part 2: Two Sub-Genres — Know Which One You're Making

### Sub-Genre A: Face Reconstruction (RelaxHistoryAI Style)

**Core format:** Historical portrait → AI photorealistic reconstruction reveal
**Emotional target:** Empathy, connection, "I'm looking at a real person"
**Topic selection:** Famous figures whose real appearance is unknown or surprising
**Visual centerpiece:** The reconstructed face — everything else is secondary
**Best performers:** Royalty, ancient rulers, founding figures, famous women, non-European historical figures

### Sub-Genre B: Location/Event Reconstruction (Farzan Films / Matt Reconstructs History Style)

**Core format:** Historical narration + cinematic AI-generated visuals of places, events, people
**Emotional target:** Awe, curiosity, "I want to know more"
**Topic selection:** Events, civilizations, turning points, daily life
**Visual centerpiece:** The historical environment — cities, battles, markets, homes
**Best performers:** Rome, Egypt, Victorian London, ancient daily life, "what X looked like before"

**This guide covers both but emphasizes the RelaxHistoryAI (face reconstruction) style.**

## Part 3: Script Structure

### The QQPP Hook Formula (First 15 Seconds)

Q — Question that creates curiosity
Q — Second question that deepens it
P — Promise of what they'll see
P — Preview that teases the reveal

**Example for face reconstruction:**
> "Do you know what Abraham Lincoln actually looked like? Not the portrait — the real person.
> Historians have debated his features for 160 years.
> We fed every photograph, painting, and physical description into AI.
> Here's what he probably actually looked like."

**Example for historical fact/law style (AI Historian):**
> "Ancient Rome had a law you won't believe existed.
> It was enforced for 500 years.
> This one law changed how the entire Roman Empire worked.
> And it applies to your life right now."

### Three-Act Structure for Longer Shorts (45-60 seconds)

**Act 1 — The Problem/Mystery (0-15s):**
- Hook immediately — no intro, no channel name
- Establish what's unknown or surprising
- Create the "open loop" (a question the viewer needs answered)

**Act 2 — The Revelation/Discovery (15-45s):**
- Deliver the reconstruction or main content
- Add supporting context — 2-3 facts that deepen the impact
- One pattern interruption every 20-30 seconds (new visual, new surprising detail)

**Act 3 — The Resonance Close (45-60s):**
- Don't fade out slowly — end with energy or a final surprising note
- One closing line that reframes everything: "This is the face that changed history."
- Optional: call to engagement ("Which figure should we reconstruct next?")

### Script Language Rules

DO:
- Use second person ("you") to make it feel direct
- Use present tense for historical events ("Caesar walks into the Senate")
- Short sentences. Max 12-15 words per sentence.
- Conversational, not academic
- Specific details over general statements ("She was 5'2" with auburn hair" vs. "She was described as attractive")
- Superlatives that are true ("one of the most powerful women in history")

DON'T:
- Never start with "Hi everyone" or channel intro
- No filler phrases ("basically," "kind of," "sort of")
- No hedging language in the hook ("Some historians believe..." — save qualifications for after the hook)
- No slow builds — the hook must be the first sentence

## Part 4: Narration/Voiceover Style

### Tone

**Target tone:** Documentary authority with human warmth. Think Ken Burns narration energy, but faster pacing and more emotional direct address.

- **NOT:** Academic lecture, dry fact recitation
- **NOT:** Over-excited YouTuber energy ("INSANE facts about Rome!!")
- **YES:** Calm authority + genuine curiosity + occasional awe

### Pacing

- **Shorts:** 2.5-3.5 words per second (faster than standard documentary)
- Pause before the reveal moment — let silence work
- Rising intonation on questions (hook lines)
- Slower, more deliberate on the payoff line
- No dead air — every second of narration should be earning its place

### AI Voice Selection (ElevenLabs or similar)

For face reconstruction (RelaxHistoryAI style):
- Use a **calm, warm, authoritative voice** — not dramatic
- Male or female both work — choose based on historical figure gender if relevant
- Voice should feel like someone who *knows* history, not someone performing history
- Avoid voices that sound robotic, sing-songy, or overly dramatic
- ElevenLabs recommended voices for this style: deep, measured, documentary tone
- Adjust pacing: slower than default, add strategic pauses before reveals

For fact-based style (AI Historian style):
- Slightly faster pace
- More animated/punchy on hook lines
- Still authoritative, not childlike

### Word Choice Patterns

High-performing opening words for history Shorts:
- "For centuries..." / "For 2,000 years..."
- "Most people don't know..."
- "In [year/era], [surprising thing happened]"
- "This is what [famous person] actually looked like."
- "The history books got this wrong."
- "Before [modern thing], there was..."

## Part 5: Visual Style

### For Face Reconstruction (RelaxHistoryAI Style)

**The Reveal Structure:**
1. Show the original source (painting, portrait, bust) — 3-5 seconds
2. Slow zoom or push in on the face
3. Crossfade or dissolve transition (NOT a hard cut, NOT a flashy wipe)
4. Hold on the reconstructed face — minimum 5-7 seconds
5. Allow the viewer to actually look at the face — this is the payoff

**Image Quality Standards:**
- Minimum 1080p, prefer 4K output
- Photorealism is non-negotiable — if the AI image looks obviously fake, regenerate
- Natural lighting only (no dramatic colored lighting — this undermines the "real photograph" illusion)
- Skin texture must be visible: pores, lines, imperfections
- Eyes must be naturalistic — AI often generates uncanny eyes; fix or regenerate
- Hair must look real — not perfect, not stylized
- Appropriate aging — if the figure was 60 at their peak, the reconstruction shows 60

**Aspect Ratio:**
- 9:16 vertical for Shorts
- Face should fill 60-70% of frame — this is a face-forward format
- Headroom: small, tight composition
- Subject centered or slight rule-of-thirds offset

**Color Grade:**
- Neutral, photorealistic — resist the urge to add heavy color grading
- Slight warmth is acceptable (feels historical without feeling artificial)
- Documentary color, not cinematic drama
- No heavy vignettes, no dark moody grades — these undermine realism
- If using a LUT, choose warm neutral presets (not high-contrast cinematic)

### For Location/Event Style (Farzan Films / Historical Reconstruction)

**Visual Flow:**
- Wide establishing shot → mid-shot → close detail → character/people level
- Match visuals precisely to narration — if you say "the markets of Rome," show markets
- Dissolve transitions between historical scenes (not jarring cuts)
- Layer ambient sound design beneath narration (marketplace noise, period sounds from Freesound.org)
- B-roll mix: AI-generated images + authentic historical photographs where available

**Color Grade for Event/Location Style:**
- Cinematic grade acceptable here (unlike face reconstruction)
- Warm grades for ancient/medieval (golden hour feel)
- Cool desaturated for modern-era events (1800s-1900s)
- High contrast for dramatic moments
- Epic/moody is fine here because the goal is awe, not realism

## Part 6: Text Overlays and Captions

### Caption Style

For face reconstruction:
- Minimal text on the reconstructed face — don't obscure it
- Name + dates as simple title card (before or after the reveal)
- Font: clean serif or simple sans-serif — nothing decorative
- Color: white or light gold on dark areas, or dark on light
- Size: readable at mobile size (test at 375px wide)
- No animated caption effects during the face reveal moment

For fact-based/narration style:
- Captions synced precisely to voiceover
- Yellow text is the current dominant style in this genre (high contrast, reads fast)
- Word-by-word or short phrase highlighting (not full sentences on screen at once)
- Font: bold sans-serif (Montserrat Bold, Arial Black, or similar)
- Positioning: bottom 20-30% of frame, centered
- Avoid placing captions over faces

### Title Cards

- Opening title: figure's name only, minimal styling
- Era/date subtitle in smaller text below
- Keep on screen 1.5-2 seconds maximum, then cut
- Don't use titles as a substitute for a hook — the narration IS the hook

## Part 7: Music and Audio

### For Face Reconstruction (RelaxHistoryAI Style)

**Ideal audio approach:**
- Subtle ambient music, very low in mix (30-40% of narration volume)
- Music should not compete with the visual reveal moment
- Consider silence or near-silence during the face reveal — let the image breathe
- Piano, strings, or sparse orchestral work well
- Avoid: dramatic epic scores (undermines the quiet "real person" feeling)
- Avoid: modern electronic music (breaks the historical illusion)

**Sources:** Epidemic Sound, Artlist, or public domain classical recordings

### For Fact/Event Style (AI Historian, Farzan Films Style)

**Ideal audio approach:**
- More prominent music, 50-60% of narration volume
- Epic orchestral/cinematic acceptable and common
- Music swells on reveals and climaxes
- Dramatic percussion on list items or surprising facts
- Match music tempo to narration energy level

**General audio rules:**
- Voice narration must always be clearest element in mix
- Use audio ducking automatically when narration is present
- No music during hook's first 2 sentences — let the words land clean
- Fade in music after hook, fade out cleanly before final line

## Part 8: Topic Selection — What Goes Viral

### Highest-Performing Topic Categories

**For face reconstruction:**
1. Founding fathers / major historical leaders (Washington, Lincoln, Caesar)
2. Ancient female rulers (Cleopatra, Nefertiti, Elizabeth I)
3. Famous murder victims or tragic figures (Anastasia Romanov, Anne Boleyn)
4. Native American / non-European leaders (Geronimo, Sitting Bull, Saladin)
5. Scientists and thinkers whose portraits are rare (Marie Curie, Tesla)
6. "Debated appearance" figures (Shakespeare, Jesus — controversial but high engagement)

**For fact/event style:**
1. Ancient daily life ("What Romans ate for breakfast")
2. Surprising/disturbing ancient laws
3. "Before/after" locations (Ancient Rome vs. today)
4. Famous figures' surprising personal habits or secrets
5. Military tactics or turning points in famous battles
6. "The real story behind..." demystification

### Topic Selection Formula

The best topics have:
- **Recognition:** Viewer already knows the name
- **Surprise:** Something about the topic most people don't know
- **Visual potential:** Can be shown, not just told
- **Emotional resonance:** Connects to something human (love, death, injustice, power)
- **Curiosity gap:** The title/hook creates a question the viewer needs answered

**The "60-second hook" test:**
Can you state the surprising thing in one sentence that makes someone stop scrolling?
- BAD: "History of the Roman Empire"
- GOOD: "Rome had a law that made every citizen legally responsible for their neighbor's debts"
- BAD: "What Cleopatra looked like"
- GOOD: "This is what Cleopatra actually looked like — and she wasn't Greek"

## Part 9: What Separates RelaxHistoryAI from Generic AI History Channels

### Generic AI History Channel Problems

Per 404 Media investigative report (September 2025), most AI history channels suffer from:
- Scripts that just repeat what's already on Wikipedia
- "Very simplified version of the past" — no academic sources
- Audio artifacts from low-quality TTS
- Historically inaccurate visuals (wrong architecture, wrong clothing period)
- Mass-produced feel — 5 videos per day, no differentiation

### What RelaxHistoryAI Does Differently

1. **One specific emotional mechanism:** Not "here are facts" but "here is a face you've never seen"
2. **Quality over quantity:** Face reconstructions require individual attention to source material
3. **Accuracy as a feature:** The reconstruction is only compelling if it seems *plausible* — this forces historical research
4. **Restraint in visuals:** The best AI history channels don't over-design; they let the image do the work
5. **The empathy trigger:** Faces activate human empathy in ways that text, maps, and landscapes don't

### The Key Differentiating Principle

> Generic AI history channel: "Here is a list of facts about Cleopatra."
> RelaxHistoryAI: "Here is what Cleopatra actually looked like."

The second format requires no narration to be compelling. The image itself is the content. Everything else — the music, the text, the pacing — is in service of that one emotional moment when the viewer looks at a photorealistic reconstruction and thinks: *that's a real person.*

## Part 10: Production Workflow (Recommended Stack)

| Step | Tool | Notes |
|------|------|-------|
| Script writing | Claude AI / ChatGPT | Use QQPP hook formula; document historical sources |
| Historical research | Academic databases, Wikipedia as starting point only | Verify every claim |
| Image generation | Midjourney v6+, Google Imagen 3, or Stable Diffusion | Multiple generations, pick best |
| Face enhancement | GFPGAN, CodeFormer, or Topaz Gigapixel | Fix AI artifacts in faces |
| Character animation | Dreamina, D-ID, HeyGen | For subtle movement/life |
| Voiceover | ElevenLabs | Documentary voice preset, slow pacing |
| Video assembly | CapCut or Filmora | Vertical 9:16, dissolve transitions |
| Captions | CapCut auto-captions or Submagic | Sync to voice, minimal styling |
| Music | Epidemic Sound, Artlist, Pixabay Music | Subtle ambient, low mix |
| Thumbnail | Canva | Comparison format: original portrait vs. reconstruction |

## Quick Reference — Style Checklist

Before publishing any history Short, verify:

- [ ] Hook lands in first 3 seconds — no intro
- [ ] First sentence creates a curiosity gap
- [ ] No sentences longer than 15 words
- [ ] Face reconstructions show natural lighting, realistic skin texture
- [ ] Reveal moment is not obscured by text overlays
- [ ] Music does not compete with narration during the hook
- [ ] Captions are legible at mobile size
- [ ] Historical accuracy verified (at minimum, no provably false claims)
- [ ] Video ends on a high-energy note, not a fade
- [ ] Thumbnail shows the most emotionally compelling image (usually the reconstructed face)
- [ ] Title creates a curiosity gap, not just a description
