## VIDEO 1
### Title: *The Man Who Found $2 Billion In Gold — And Died Broke*
**Hook:**
On January 24, 1848, a carpenter named James Marshall picked up a small shiny rock from a ditch in California. That moment launched the most famous gold rush in history — and completely destroyed his life.

**Why it performs:**
The "person ruined by their own discovery" narrative is universally compelling. Irony drives shares. Title creates an immediate paradox that demands an explanation. Performs like a biographical tragedy with a twist ending.

**Key facts to cover:**
- Marshall was a carpenter, not a prospector — accidental discovery
- Sutter tried to keep it secret; the secret lasted days
- Within months, 300,000 people invaded the area
- Miners attacked Marshall and drove him from his own discovery site
- Marshall spent years petitioning Congress for recognition and payment — received nothing
- Sutter: empire destroyed, died in poverty also
- The land where gold was found today generates millions in tourism — none of it went to either man
- Bonus: John Sutter tried to claim land rights, was ignored by every court

- Workers building modern office towers still dig up ships
- San Francisco Maritime National Historical Park has mapped 70+ confirmed locations
- Some ships still contain cargo — unopened barrels, tools, personal belongings
- The city literally grew on top of abandoned dreams

- Foreign Miners' Tax (1850): $20/month imposed specifically on Chinese, Mexican, Chilean miners
- ~120,000 Native Americans died of disease, starvation, and homicide during the rush
- U.S. government removed survivors to reservations — often hundreds of miles from their homes
- Governor Newsom's 2019 acknowledgment of genocide — but no reparations, no land returned
- Parallel to Black Hills: gold rush consistently required Native displacement

## VIDEO 8
### Title: *The Miners' Rebellion That Created Australian Democracy*
**Hook:**
On December 3, 1854, 276 British soldiers attacked 150 sleeping gold miners in Ballarat, Australia. The miners had built a wooden fort, raised a flag, and made a list of demands. What happened next changed the entire country.

**Why it performs:**
Underdog vs. empire narrative. Eureka Stockade is Australia's founding democratic myth — massive search volume in Australia. International audiences are largely unaware of this story. Clean 3-act structure (grievance → rebellion → victory) makes for a perfect video.

**Key facts to cover:**
- Australia discovered gold in 1851 — almost simultaneously as California wound down
- Victoria produced 40% of the world's gold in the 1850s
- Mining license fees: 30 shillings/month whether you found gold or not
- "Digger hunts" — police enforcement raids, humiliating arrests
- October 1854 trigger: James Scobie murdered; Bentley acquitted by corrupt courts; miners burned the hotel
- November 29, 1854: miners raised Southern Cross flag, built the Eureka Stockade
- December 3, 1854: pre-dawn military attack on sleeping miners; 27 killed (mostly rebels)
- 13 arrested for high treason — all acquitted by juries
- Within 12 months: mining licenses abolished, voting rights extended, democracy advanced
- The Eureka flag is still used today in Australian political protest
- Welcome Stranger nugget (1869): 70 kg, found 3 cm below surface, had to be broken to fit on bank scales — worth ~$11.6 million at today's prices

- Modern visitors can still pan for small amounts of gold in many locations

## VIDEO 10
### Title: *The Avalanche That Killed 65 Gold Miners In A Single Morning*
**Hook:**
On the morning of April 3, 1898, hundreds of men were climbing a staircase cut into the ice of a Alaskan mountain, each carrying as much weight as they possibly could. Then the mountain came down on top of them.

**Why it performs:**
Disaster narrative with vivid imagery. The Palm Sunday Avalanche is one of the most dramatic single events of any gold rush and is underreported. Visceral, tragic, visually powerful for video. The "1 ton of supplies, 30–40 trips" setup builds enormous dramatic tension before the disaster.

**Key facts to cover:**
- Canadian government required each stampeder to bring 1 ton of supplies
- The Chilkoot Trail: 33 miles, 3,759-foot elevation at the pass
- "Golden Staircase": 1,500 steps cut into ice; climbers used a guide rope
- Many climbers made 30–40 trips each, walking 4,000+ km total just hauling supplies
- April 3, 1898: Palm Sunday — five avalanche slides
- 65+ killed; 47 bodies recovered from the snow
- Rescuers dug for days; the sound of screaming under the snow eventually stopped
- Other dangers: White Pass trail was so muddy and brutal, horses died by the hundreds — nicknamed "Dead Horse Trail"
- Starvation along the route; scurvy; typhoid
- 100,000 tried; only 30,000–40,000 reached Dawson; only hundreds got rich
- The entire journey was for almost nothing for most participants

## VIDEO 11
### Title: *California's Gold Rush Poisoned the State's Water — And Will For The Next 10,000 Years*
**Hook:**
The California Gold Rush ended in 1855. The damage it caused to the environment won't be fully cleaned up until the year 12,000 — if ever. Right now, beneath the Sierra Nevada, between 10 and 13 million pounds of mercury are slowly poisoning the rivers, the fish, and the people.

**Why it performs:**
Environmental angle brings in a completely different audience (eco-conscious viewers, science channels, modern-issues viewers). The "10,000 years" stat is immediately shocking and quotable. Connects past events to present-day consequences — very shareable. Educational without being preachy.

**Key facts to cover:**
- Mercury (quicksilver) used in amalgamation process to extract gold from ore
- 10–13 million pounds of mercury discharged into California's environment during the rush
- Still exceeds EPA thresholds in Sierra Nevada waterways today
- Malakoff Diggins: 100 grams per year still flowing into Yuba River watershed
- Fish, birds, and organisms near historically mined sites have measurably higher mercury
- 10,000-year contamination timeline — no practical remediation exists
- Hydraulic mining: high-pressure water jets blasted hillsides apart
- Billions of cubic yards of sediment dumped into rivers
- Sacramento Valley farmland buried under mining debris
- 1884 Sawyer Decision: first major environmental court ruling in U.S. history — banned hydraulic mining
- Today: hundreds of Superfund-adjacent sites in California trace to the gold rush
- This is the largest legacy environmental disaster most Americans have never heard of

**B-roll search terms:**
- Mercury contamination water California mining
- Hydraulic mining historical California erosion
- Sierra Nevada river watershed aerial
- Malakoff Diggins hydraulic mine California
- Environmental contamination soil water testing

"The one you don't know about" is one of the highest-performing history video formats. - The Eureka Stockade — miners' rebellion that created Australian democracy
- Peter Lalor (Eureka leader) later became a parliamentarian — went from outlaw to lawmaker
