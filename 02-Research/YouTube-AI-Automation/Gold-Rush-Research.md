## 1. California Gold Rush 1848–1855

### Discovery
- **Date:** January 24, 1848 — James W. Marshall found gold in the tailrace of Sutter's Mill, Coloma, California
- Marshall was a carpenter building the mill, not even a prospector
- He picked up the nugget and said: *"My eye was caught by something shining in the bottom of the ditch...I reached my hand down and picked it up; it made my heart thump, for I was certain it was gold."*
- Sutter desperately tried to keep the discovery secret — he feared it would ruin his agricultural empire plans
- News leaked immediately. By 1849 it was global.

### Scale of the Rush
- Approximately **300,000 people** arrived in California from 1848–1855
- 90,000 arrived in 1849 alone — the "Forty-Niners"
- About 50,000–60,000 were American; the rest came from Mexico, China, Australia, Europe, and South America
- ~95% were men; gender ratio peaked at **12.2 men per woman** in 1850
- San Francisco grew from ~200 residents in 1846 to **36,000 by 1852**

### Gold Extracted
| Year | Value |
|------|-------|
| 1849 | $10 million |
| 1850 | $41 million |
| 1851 | $75 million |
| 1852 | $81 million (peak) |
| 1855+ | ~$45 million/year (declining) |

- **Total extracted:** estimated $2 billion (in 1800s dollars)
- At the peak, individual lucky strikes yielded chunks of gold weighing up to **195 pounds**

### The Bitter Ironies
- **James Marshall** — the man who discovered California gold — died penniless. Prospectors attacked and drove him from the site of his discovery. His estate barely covered his funeral expenses.
- **John Sutter** — the mill owner whose land birthed the rush — had his agricultural empire destroyed when his workers abandoned everything for gold. He spent the rest of his life petitioning Congress for compensation. Never received a cent.

### Inflation and Prices (1849)
These prices in today's equivalent dollars:
- 1 dozen eggs: **$90**
- Slice of buttered bread: **$56**
- 1 lb of coffee: **$1,200**
- A new pair of boots: **$3,000**
- A pickaxe: **$1,500**
- Sam Brannan's gold pans: bought for $0.20, resold for $15 each (75x markup)

### Ships Under San Francisco
- Nearly 1,000 ships arrived in San Francisco harbor during the rush
- Crews and passengers abandoned their ships the moment they docked — everyone ran for the gold fields
- Over **70 ships** are now buried under the streets of San Francisco's Financial District
- The city was built on landfill over these abandoned vessels
- Ships discovered include the *Niantic* (near the Transamerica Pyramid), the *General Harrison*, and dozens more
- This is one of the most underreported gold rush facts

### Death Valley Name Origin
- Death Valley got its name in 1849
- **13 prospectors** died trying to cross the valley en route to the gold fields
- Before dying, one allegedly looked back and said: *"Goodbye, Death Valley"*

## 2. Klondike Gold Rush 1896–1899

### Discovery
- **Date:** August 16, 1896 — gold discovered at Rabbit Creek (renamed Bonanza Creek), near Dawson, Yukon, Canada
- Discovered by: Skookum Jim Mason (Keish) and Tagish Charlie (Káa Goox), Indigenous Yukon people, along with American prospector George Carmack
- The original locals staked claims first — they were the actual Klondike Kings

### Scale of the Stampede
- Estimated **100,000 people** attempted to reach the Klondike
- Only **30,000–40,000** actually arrived in Dawson City
- Dawson City population: 500 in 1896 → **17,000 by summer 1898**
- Of the 40,000 who reached Dawson, only **~15,000** actually worked the gold fields
- Of those, only **a quarter found any gold at all**
- Only **a handful** became truly wealthy
- George Carmack left the Yukon with $1 million in gold — one of the few who actually got rich

### The Gold Numbers
- $29 million extracted in peak years 1897–1899 (unadjusted)
- Total since 1896 (to 2013): estimated **14–20 million ounces**
- Gold mining production peaked in 1903 after heavy equipment arrived

### The Journey — A Death March
Two main routes:
1. **Chilkoot Trail** — 33 miles / 53 km, starting at Dyea, Alaska. Peak point: 3,759 feet elevation.
2. **White Pass Trail** — starting at Skagway, Alaska. Wider but equally brutal.

- Stampeders had to make the Chilkoot ascent **30–40 times** to haul the mandatory 1 ton of supplies
- The Chilkoot's "Golden Staircase" = 1,500 steps cut directly into the snow and ice
- By the time they relayed all supplies, many had walked **4,000 km total**
- The North West Mounted Police required everyone to bring 1 ton of supplies or be turned back

### The Palm Sunday Avalanche — April 3, 1898
- The deadliest single event of the Klondike rush
- Five avalanche slides on the Chilkoot Trail between Sheep Camp and the Scales
- **Over 65 people killed** in a single day
- 47 bodies were recovered from the snow
- Miners ahead and behind the avalanche zone were unaware what had happened until the screaming stopped

### Soapy Smith — Crime Boss of Skagway
- Jefferson "Soapy" Smith, a con man from Denver, took control of Skagway, Alaska
- Ran a gang of **300 men** — called them his "lambs" (his victims)
- Set up a fake telegraph office — wires went nowhere — but charged Klondikers to send messages home
- Ran saloons, casinos, and fraud operations out of "Jeff's Place"
- His reign ended on **July 8, 1898**, when he was shot dead in a gunfight
- His killer, Frank Reid, also died from wounds 12 days later

### Jack London
- Arrived in the Klondike in 1897, age 21
- Found almost no gold
- Returned to California in 1898 suffering from **scurvy** (teeth loosened, legs swollen)
- The Klondike experience became his gold mine — *The Call of the Wild*, dozens of short stories
- Became the highest-paid writer in the world based on what he observed failing to get rich

### Robert Service
- Arrived after the rush was over, in 1904, as a bank teller
- His poems about the Klondike (*"The Shooting of Dan McGrew"*, *"The Cremation of Sam McGee"*) made him more famous than any gold found there
- Moral: the pen was mightier than the pickaxe

## 3. Australian Gold Rush 1851+

### Discovery
- **May 12, 1851** — Edward Hargraves and John Lister discovered gold near Bathurst, New South Wales
- Within months, gold was found in Victoria: Clunes (July 1851), Ballarat (August/September 1851), Castlemaine, Bendigo (November 1851)
- Notably, some researchers claim gold had been found earlier but suppressed — authorities feared a repeat of the Californian chaos

### Scale and Impact
- Victoria's population: **75,000 in 1851 → 500,000 in 1861** (more than 6x growth in 10 years)
- Over **19,000 Chinese miners** from Guangdong province were working Victorian goldfields by end of 1855
- Immigration from Britain, Ireland, America, Germany, China, and across the Pacific transformed Australia permanently

### The Gold Numbers
- Victoria alone produced **174 tonnes (5.6 million oz)** in 1852
- Peak year (1856): **3,053,744 troy ounces** extracted from Victoria
- Total Victorian production 1851–1896: **61,034,682 oz (1,898,391 kg)**
- During the 1850s, Australian colonies produced **40%+ of the world's gold supply**
- At peak: **2 tonnes of gold per week** flowed into the Melbourne Treasury

### Welcome Stranger — The World's Largest Gold Nugget
- Found by: John Deason and Richard Oates (Cornish miners)
- **Date:** February 5, 1869, at Moliagul, Victoria
- Found only **3 cm below the surface** — they literally almost stepped over it
- Weight: **70.57 kg smelted gold** (2,269 troy oz)
- It was **too large to fit on the bank's scales** — had to be broken on an anvil first
- Paid out approximately £9,381 (~A$1.5 million in 2022 terms; ~US$11.6 million at 2026 gold prices)
- Melted down almost immediately, sent to the Bank of England as ingots

### The Eureka Stockade — December 3, 1854
- The Australian gold rush produced a **rebellion** that shaped the entire nation
- Miners at Ballarat were forced to pay oppressive mining license fees (~30 shillings/month regardless of whether they found gold)
- Police enforced fees brutally — "digger hunts" where miners without licenses were arrested on the spot
- **October 7, 1854** — trigger event: Scottish miner James Scobie was killed outside the Eureka Hotel. The proprietor, James Bentley, was quickly acquitted by corrupt courts. Miners burned the hotel down in protest.
- **November 29, 1854** — miners erected a stockade at Eureka diggings and raised the Southern Cross flag
- **December 3, 1854** — before dawn, 276 troops attacked ~150 sleeping miners
- Casualties: **27 killed** (mostly rebels), dozens wounded
- 13 miners arrested for high treason — all acquitted by juries who refused to convict
- **Result:** Within 12 months, virtually all miner demands were met. Mining licenses replaced with affordable miner's rights. Voting rights extended. The Eureka Stockade is considered the birth of Australian democracy.

### Anti-Chinese Violence
- Chinese miners worked in organized groups of 30–100, which made them very efficient and financially successful
- White European miners resented their success
- **Buckland River Riot, Victoria — July 1857:** A mob of ~90 Europeans attacked a Chinese camp, burned tents and a Chinese temple, drove 2,500 Chinese people from their homes
- **Lambing Flat Riots, NSW — 1860–61:** Wave of anti-Chinese attacks; thousands descended on Chinese quarters, plundering, beating, robbing fleeing miners. Several killed.
- Result: NSW passed the **Chinese Immigration Act 1861**, severely restricting Chinese immigration — seeds of the later White Australia Policy

## 4. Brazilian Gold Rush — Minas Gerais 1690s–1750s

### Discovery and Origins
- ~**1694–1695** — alluvial gold discovered in what is now Minas Gerais state, Brazil (then a Portuguese colony)
- Manuel Borba Gato discovered gold at Rio das Velhas in 1695
- Found by *bandeirantes* — Brazilian-born adventurers who explored the interior
- This launched what became the **world's longest gold rush** and the largest gold mines in South America

### Scale and Migration
- Between 1693 and 1720: **400,000 Portuguese immigrants** relocated to southeastern Brazil
- Also: **500,000 enslaved Africans** were brought to the mines — the labor force that actually dug most of the gold
- By 1725, **half of Brazil's entire population** lived in Minas Gerais
- **Ouro Preto (Vila Rica)** — "Black Gold" — became the most populous city in Latin America by 1730 (~40,000 people; 80,000 by 1750)
- At its height, Ouro Preto was **larger than New York City or Boston**

### The Gold Numbers
- At peak production: estimated **350,000 oz of gold per year**
- Brazil produced ~**85% of the world's gold** during the 18th century
- Gold became Brazil's principal export for over 50 years

### Who Got the Gold: Portugal and England
- Portugal required **one-fifth (the "quinto") of all gold** to be paid to the colonial government
- 90% of exported Brazilian gold flowed to Portugal — then immediately flowed onward to pay Portugal's trade deficit with **England and France**
- Result: Brazilian gold essentially **funded Britain's Industrial Revolution**
- Portugal's domestic gold stock rose from 1.149 billion reis (1720) to 58 billion reis by mid-century — but most was spent on imports, not reinvestment
- The Baroque churches and monuments of Ouro Preto were built with this wealth

### The Slaves Who Mined the Gold
- Enslaved Africans performed virtually all the dangerous mining labor
- Over the course of the 18th century, the slave trade to Minas Gerais was massive
- Slaves had no legal claim to any gold they found
- Occasionally a slave who found a particularly large nugget would be granted freedom by their owner — a rare and entirely discretionary "reward"

### The Independence Connection — Tiradentes
- As gold production declined in the late 1700s, Portugal intensified taxation
- The threat of the *derrama* — forced collection of all unpaid gold taxes — sparked a rebellion
- **Inconfidência Mineira (1789)** — an independence conspiracy inspired by the American Revolution and French Enlightenment philosophy
- Leader: Joaquim José da Silva Xavier, known as **"Tiradentes"** (Tooth-Puller — his day job)
- The conspiracy was betrayed before it launched
- Tiradentes was the only conspirator executed — **hanged and quartered on April 21, 1792**
- His death made him a martyr; April 21 is now a Brazilian national holiday
- This all traces back directly to gold rush politics

### Legacy
- Ouro Preto is now a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** for its Baroque colonial architecture
- The wealth extracted from Minas Gerais shaped Portugal, Brazil, Britain, and the entire Atlantic economy
- It is arguably the **least-known major gold rush in history** despite being the largest in absolute gold production for its era

## 5. Black Hills Gold Rush 1874–1877

### Context: Sacred Land and a Broken Promise
- **1868 Treaty of Fort Laramie** — the U.S. government granted the Sioux Nation permanent ownership of the Black Hills ("Paha Sapa"), which were considered the sacred center of the universe by the Lakota Sioux
- The treaty explicitly stated no whites could enter without Sioux permission
- It was one of the most solemn promises the U.S. government ever made to Native Americans
- It lasted **6 years**

### Custer's Expedition — 1874
- **July–August 1874** — General George Armstrong Custer led the Black Hills Expedition: 1,000 men, officially to scout for a fort location
- The real mission: investigate reports of gold
- On August 2, 1874, the expedition discovered gold **"right from the grass roots"** — Custer sent word to the New York Times
- Word spread instantly. By fall 1874, **15,000+ prospectors** had flooded Dakota Territory, illegally crossing into treaty-protected Sioux land

### The President Looks Away
- November 3, 1875 — President Ulysses S. Grant met with his cabinet and secretly decided **the Army would no longer enforce the treaty**
- No public announcement was made — but orders went out telling soldiers to stop removing miners
- It was government-sanctioned theft, dressed up in silence

### Deadwood
- 1876 — miners moved north into the Black Hills, found a gulch full of dead trees and a creek full of gold
- **Deadwood** sprang up almost overnight
- The town existed entirely on **stolen land** — not part of any U.S. territory, not subject to U.S. laws
- Wild Bill Hickok arrived in Deadwood, July 1876 — shot dead **weeks later** while playing poker, holding aces and eights (the Dead Man's Hand)
- Calamity Jane made her name in Deadwood; buried next to Hickok at Mount Moriah Cemetery
- George Hearst arrived in 1877, bought the **Homestake Mine** for $70,000

### The Great Sioux War of 1876
- Lakota resistance to treaty violations led to full-scale war
- **June 25, 1876 — Battle of Little Bighorn**: Custer and the entire 7th Cavalry attacked a massive Sioux and Cheyenne encampment
- Sitting Bull, Crazy Horse, and Gall commanded the Native forces
- Custer and ~268 soldiers were killed — the U.S. military's worst defeat in the Indian Wars
- The "victory" was short-lived: the U.S. sent massive reinforcements; by 1877, Crazy Horse surrendered. He was murdered by soldiers while in custody.

### Forced Land Cession
- The U.S. threatened to withhold winter food rations from the Sioux unless they signed over the Black Hills
- **Under threat of starvation**, the Lakota surrendered the Black Hills in fall 1876
- This was not a treaty — it was coercion

### The Homestake Mine — America's Longest-Producing Gold Mine
- Purchased by George Hearst and partners for $70,000 in 1877
- Eventually became the **deepest mine in the United States** at 8,000 feet
- Produced more than **41 million ounces of gold** before closing in 2001
- At its peak, produced **10% of the world's gold supply**
- Generated $80 million in revenues in its first 20 years
- George Hearst's fortune from this mine funded his son **William Randolph Hearst's** media empire
- The Citizen Kane origin story begins with stolen Sioux land

### Legal Outcome
- **1980 — U.S. Supreme Court** ruled the U.S. government had acted in "bad faith" toward the Sioux
- Awarded $106 million in compensation
- The Sioux **refused the money** — they want the land back, not a check
- As of today (2026), the money sits in an account, **now worth over $2 billion** — uncollected
- The Sioux still own no part of the Black Hills

## 6. Who Actually Got Rich (Spoiler: Not the Miners)

### The Real Gold Rush Winners

**Sam Brannan — California's First Millionaire**
- Mormon newspaper publisher and shopkeeper
- Learned about the gold discovery a week before he "broke the news" to San Francisco
- Used that week to buy up **every pick, shovel, and mining pan in the city**
- Had been buying gold pans at $0.20 each and reselling them for **$15 each** (75x markup)
- His store made **$5,000/day** at peak ($155,000+ in 2020 dollars)
- By 1860, California's wealthiest man
- Ironic ending: he went broke. His wife divorced him in 1870 and the settlement forced him to liquidate everything. He died in poverty at 70.

**Levi Strauss**
- Bavarian immigrant who came to San Francisco in 1850 intending to sell canvas for tents
- Miners needed durable pants more than canvas
- Created riveted denim work pants — **blue jeans**
- His invention outlasted every gold rush by 170+ years
- Levi Strauss & Co. is still worth billions today

**Philip Armour (Armour Meatpacking)**
- Came to California gold rush as a young man
- Made his fortune not mining but **running a butcher shop** in Sacramento
- Parlayed gold rush profits into the meatpacking empire that bears his name

**Henry Wells and William Fargo — Wells Fargo**
- Founded **March 18, 1852** in New York City, specifically to capitalize on the California Gold Rush
- Provided banking and express services for prospectors — buying, selling, transporting gold dust
- Became the dominant bank, mail carrier, stagecoach company, and express agency in the American West
- Wells Fargo today is one of the largest banks in the world — its origin is the gold rush

**George Hearst**
- Not a California gold rush winner but a Black Hills winner
- Bought Homestake Mine in 1877 for $70,000
- Eventually worth $200–400 million at death in 1891
- His wealth funded his son **William Randolph Hearst** — the most powerful media mogul in American history

**John Studebaker**
- Came to California gold fields and made wheelbarrows for miners — not gold
- Saved enough to go back to Indiana and co-found **Studebaker**, which became one of America's most famous wagon and later automobile companies

**Charles Crocker, Leland Stanford, Collis Huntington, Mark Hopkins (The Big Four)**
- All got rich selling supplies, real estate, and services to miners — then used that gold rush wealth to build the **Transcontinental Railroad**
- Stanford University is named after one of them — founded on gold rush supply profits

### The Pattern
In every gold rush, the same formula repeated:
1. Miners rush in chasing easy wealth
2. Almost none of them find significant gold
3. Prices for everything skyrocket (basic survival costs a fortune)
4. Merchants, suppliers, bankers, land speculators, and entertainers capture the real wealth
5. The miners leave poorer than they arrived

## 7. Dark History — Deaths, Disease, Violence, Genocide

### California Gold Rush

**Native American Genocide**
- Before the gold rush, California had an estimated **150,000 Native Americans**
- By 1870: **only 30,000 remained**
- In 20 years, **80% of California's Native population was killed**
- Methods: massacre by colonists, starvation, disease, forced labor, kidnapping, enslavement
- The California state government **paid bounties** for Native American scalps and severed heads:
  - $5 per severed Indian head in Shasta (1855)
  - 25 cents per scalp in Honey Lake (1863)
- California raised **$1.51 million** in state funds to pay for militia expeditions against Native Americans
- Between 1846 and 1873: settlers killed **9,492–16,094** California Natives directly; thousands more died of starvation or were worked to death
- Indigenous children were kidnapped and sold at auctions for **$50–$250 each**
- In June 2019, Governor Gavin Newsom officially acknowledged the state's role in genocide — 170 years late

**Forty-Niners' Death Rate**
- **1 in 12 forty-niners perished**
- Cholera, dysentery, yellow fever, tuberculosis, scurvy, measles, diphtheria were all rampant
- No sanitation infrastructure whatsoever — open sewage, contaminated water
- Cholera killed approximately **3% of all gold rushers** on the overland trail alone
- The 2,000-mile overland route saw cholera kill far more than any other cause

**Violence and Crime**
- The gender ratio (12.2 men per woman) and the combination of gold, alcohol, and desperation created explosive violence
- Crime rates were extraordinarily high; vigilantism was the only law enforcement in many areas
- Vigilante committees in San Francisco publicly hanged dozens of men without trials

### Klondike Gold Rush
- Death from: hypothermia, avalanche, starvation, scurvy, typhoid, dysentery
- The Palm Sunday Avalanche (April 3, 1898): 65+ killed in a single day
- Jack London returned with scurvy so severe his teeth were loosening
- Indigenous Hän people of the Yukon: forcibly removed from their land to make way for Klondikers, many died
- Dawson City suffered multiple devastating fires

### Australian Gold Rush
- Lambing Flat Riots (1860–61): several Chinese miners killed; thousands displaced
- Buckland River Riot (1857): Chinese camp burned, 2,500 displaced
- Eureka Stockade (1854): 27 killed in military assault on sleeping miners
- Ongoing violence between miners of different nationalities

### Brazilian Gold Rush
- **500,000 enslaved Africans** provided the primary labor force
- Enslaved workers who escaped faced brutal recapture and punishment
- The derrama (forced tax collection) created a reign of terror
- Tiradentes — independence leader — was hanged and his body quartered (each limb sent to a different town as a warning)

## 8. Ghost Towns and Environmental Devastation

### The Abandoned Towns

**Bodie, California**
- Gold discovered 1859; population peaked at **10,000+ by 1879**
- By 1882 the gold was largely gone; companies went bankrupt
- 1932: massive fire burned 90% of the town
- Last mine closed 1942
- Now a **state historic park** — preserved in "arrested decay" — roofs caving, furniture still inside, calendars still on the wall
- One of the best-preserved ghost towns in America

**Calico, California** (silver, not gold — but gold rush era)
- 500 mines, population of 3,500
- Silver price regulation in 1896 made mines unprofitable overnight
- Ghost town by 1906

**Dawson City, Yukon**
- Population 30,000+ at peak (1898)
- Dropped to a few hundred after gold exhausted and prospectors left for Nome, Alaska (1899)
- Today's population: ~1,400

**Ghost Towns Across the West**
- Estimates suggest over **300 ghost towns** in California alone
- Many sites were entirely abandoned within 5–10 years of the gold running out

### Environmental Devastation — The 10,000-Year Legacy

**Mercury Poisoning — California**
- Mercury (quicksilver) was used extensively to extract gold from ore (amalgamation process)
- Estimated **10–13 million pounds of mercury** were discharged into California's environment
- Mercury persists in riverbeds, soils, and fish tissue
- Still exceeds EPA thresholds in Sierra Nevada waterways today
- From Malakoff Diggins alone: **100 grams of mercury per year** still flows into the Yuba River watershed
- This contamination is expected to persist for **10,000 years** — no practical cleanup exists

**Hydraulic Mining**
- High-pressure water jets ("monitors") blasted entire hillsides to expose gold-bearing gravels
- Dumped billions of cubic yards of sediment into California rivers
- Sacramento Valley farmland was buried under several feet of mining debris
- The legal battle over hydraulic mining ended in **1884 (Sawyer Decision)** — the first major environmental court ruling in U.S. history

**Deforestation**
- Massive logging to fuel boilers, build mine shafts, and construct boomtowns
- Sierra Nevada foothills were substantially stripped of forest cover

**Australia**
- Victorian goldfields suffered massive landscape destruction
- Rivers redirected, wetlands drained, forests cleared
